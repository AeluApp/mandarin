The one thing I wish I'd done from day one: **read graded Chinese passages from the very first week.**

I spent my first two months doing flashcards exclusively. My vocabulary grew, but I couldn't read anything real. I could recognize 好 on a card but not in a sentence. These are different skills, and flashcards only train one of them.

Graded readers exist at every HSK level — even HSK 1, with passages using only ~150 words. They're simple, but they're real Chinese in sentence form. Start reading earlier than you think you're ready.

The bonus: when you look up a word while reading, you've encountered it in context. If you then drill that word, the memory is richer. This is what I call the "cleanup loop," and it's the core of the app I'm building.

## Issue 2: The Forgetting Curve
**Subject:** Why you forget 60% of new vocabulary in 48 hours (and what to do about it)

Hey —

Quick science lesson that changed how I study:

In 1885, Hermann Ebbinghaus memorized lists of nonsense syllables and tracked how quickly he forgot them. The result: the "forgetting curve." Without review, you lose ~60% of new material within 48 hours and ~90% within a month.

The fix isn't "study harder." It's **study at the right time.**

Spaced repetition works by testing you right before you'd forget. The intervals expand as the memory strengthens: 1 day → 3 days → 7 days → 21 days → 60 days. Get it wrong, and the interval shrinks.

Over time, you spend 80% of your study time on the 20% of words you're weakest on. No wasted reps reviewing words you already know.

This is especially powerful for Chinese, where you're looking at 3,000-5,000+ unique characters. Without SRS, the volume is simply unmanageable.

The practical takeaway: **review new words within 24 hours of first encountering them.** If you read a passage today and looked up 5 words, review those 5 words tomorrow. Don't wait until your "next study session" three days from now.

— Mandarin

## Issue 3: The Listening Gap
**Subject:** My vocabulary is HSK 3. My listening is HSK 2. Here's why.

Hey —

I ran diagnostics on my Chinese skills last week. The results were humbling.

Vocabulary recognition: solidly HSK 3.
Reading comprehension: borderline HSK 3.
Listening comprehension: barely HSK 2.
Tone accuracy: embarrassingly close to HSK 1.

If I'd assessed my "level" based on vocabulary alone (like most apps do), I'd have said "I'm HSK 3." But the listening section of the actual HSK 3 test would have destroyed me.

**The skills diverge.** This is the most important thing I've learned about language study. Your "level" isn't one number — it's four.

The fix was straightforward: I started spending 10 minutes a day on listening-specific practice. Graded passages at my level, played at natural speed, no transcript visible. After listening, I reveal the transcript and check what I missed.

Three weeks in, the gap is closing. Not dramatically — listening is genuinely slow to build. But it's measurable.

The lesson: if you're only tracking vocabulary count, you're flying blind. Track each skill separately and let your study time follow the weakest one.

— Mandarin

## Issue 4: Tone Pairs
**Subject:** The 10-minute drill that improved my Chinese listening more than anything

Hey —

If I could recommend one specific practice to every Mandarin learner, it's this: **tone pair drills.**

Not individual tones — pairs. In natural speech, you almost never hear a tone in isolation. You hear sequences: 2nd + 3rd, 1st + 4th, 3rd + 3rd.

Some combinations are easy for English speakers. Some are nightmares. The hard ones for me:

- **2nd + 3rd** (rising then dipping): rénmín 人民. I kept hearing both syllables as 2nd tone.
- **3rd + 3rd** (tone sandhi): nǐhǎo 你好 → actually pronounced níhǎo. The rule is simple; hearing it in real time is not.
- **4th + 2nd** (falling then rising): dàxué 大学. The transition between a sharp fall and a rise trips me up.

I started drilling these specific combinations for 10 minutes a day. Not passively listening — actively identifying. After about 3 weeks, I noticed I was catching more in natural-speed audio.

The research backs this up: discrimination between similar sounds improves with targeted practice. Tone pairs are to Mandarin what minimal pairs are to any phonologically challenging language.

If you do nothing else this week, find a tone pair drill (many apps and websites have them) and spend 10 minutes on it. You'll feel the difference.

— Mandarin

P.S. Which tone combination trips you up most? For most English speakers it's 2nd + 3rd.

## Issue 5: HSK 3.0 Changes
**Subject:** HSK is getting overhauled in July 2026 — here's what matters

Hey —

Big news if you're studying for HSK: the entire test is being restructured.

**What's changing:**
- Levels expand from 1-6 to 1-9
- HSKK (speaking test) becomes **mandatory** from Level 3 onward (currently optional)
- Vocabulary lists are being reorganized significantly
- Reading and listening sections are weighted more heavily

**What this means for you right now:**

If you're studying for HSK 3 or above, **start practicing speaking.** The old test let you pass without saying a word. The new one won't.

Specifically: tone accuracy in spoken production. Not just recognizing tones — producing them correctly. This is a different skill and most learners are weaker at it than they think.

The transition timeline: old HSK tests will be phased out through 2026-2027. Check your local test center for specific dates.

**My recommendation:** Don't panic. If you're building strong fundamentals (vocabulary + listening + reading + tones), you're preparing for ANY version of the test. The learners who will struggle are the ones who over-indexed on vocabulary lists and neglected the other skills.

The new HSK is actually a better test. It measures real proficiency, not just word recognition.

— Mandarin

## Issue 6: The Cleanup Loop Deep Dive
**Subject:** Read → look up → drill → repeat (the study cycle that actually works)

Hey —

I've mentioned the "cleanup loop" a few times. Today I want to explain exactly how it works and why.

**The cycle:**
1. Read a Chinese passage at your level
2. When you hit a word you don't know, look it up (tap for pinyin + definition)
3. The system logs every word you looked up
4. Your next drill session prioritizes those specific words
5. After drilling, go back to reading

**Why this works better than standard SRS:**

Most SRS apps give you a curriculum — "here are the 600 words for HSK 3, drill them." The problem: these are words the average HSK 3 learner needs. Not necessarily YOUR gaps.

The cleanup loop inverts this. Instead of drilling a generic list, you're drilling the specific words YOU struggled with in the specific contexts where you encountered them.

When I drill 看见 after reading a story where someone "看见了一个老朋友" (saw an old friend), the association is richer than drilling 看见 from a wordlist. The context comes for free.

**The research angle:** this maps to what cognitive scientists call "desirable difficulty" — the words are harder because you genuinely didn't know them, and that difficulty makes the learning more durable.

I've been using this cycle for [X] weeks and the difference in retention is noticeable. Words from reading stick. Words from lists... less so.

— Mandarin

## Issue 7: What I Got Wrong
**Subject:** Three mistakes I made building a Chinese learning app

Hey —

I've been building this app for a while now, and I want to be honest about what I got wrong. Not because failure stories are trendy — because these mistakes probably apply to how you study too.

**Mistake 1: Features over content.**
I built 27 drill types before I had great content to drill. Having a tone pair drill is useless if the word data doesn't include tone information. I should have spent 70% of my time on content quality and 30% on code. I did the opposite.

**Lesson for learners:** The quality of your study material matters more than the tool you use. A well-curated Anki deck beats a fancy app with bad data.

**Mistake 2: Ignoring listening for too long.**
I built vocabulary drills first because they were easy to implement. Listening features came months later. My own listening suffered as a result.

**Lesson for learners:** Don't skip the hard skills just because they're harder to practice. Listening is uncomfortable. Do it anyway.

**Mistake 3: Measuring the wrong things.**
For weeks, I tracked "items drilled per day." This is a vanity metric. It measures effort, not learning. I switched to tracking retention rate (can you recall this word 7 days later?) and everything changed.

**Lesson for learners:** "I studied for 2 hours" means nothing. "I can recognize 85% of HSK 3 vocabulary in context" means something.

— Mandarin

## Issue 8: One Year of Mandarin (When You Reach This Milestone)
**Subject:** What [X] months of Mandarin actually looks like — the honest version

Hey —

I've been studying Mandarin for [X] months. Here's the honest report.

**What I can do:**
- Read HSK [X]-level passages slowly but with comprehension
- Understand graded audio at 0.8-1.0x speed
- Hold a very simple conversation about daily topics
- Write basic messages in Chinese
- Recognize [X] characters reliably

**What I can't do:**
- Follow a Chinese TV show without subtitles
- Have an unscripted conversation beyond surface topics
- Read a newspaper
- Understand regional accents
- Produce correct tones consistently under pressure

**What surprised me:**
- Reading improved faster than anything else. Characters compound.
- Listening improved slowest. Still my weakest skill.
- Tones are a lifelong practice, not a skill you "master"
- 15 minutes daily was more effective than 2-hour weekend sessions
- The emotional journey is real — long plateaus with sudden breakthroughs

**What I'd tell someone starting today:**
1. Start reading graded content immediately
2. Track skills separately (vocabulary, listening, reading, tones)
3. Drill what you actually struggle with, not generic word lists
4. 15 minutes daily, every day, no exceptions
5. Be honest with yourself about your level

This is a multi-year project. I'm at the beginning. But the beginning is further than I expected to get.
