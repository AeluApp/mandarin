# Brand Narrative -- Mandarin

This is the emotional identity document for Mandarin. It defines who the brand is at the level of feeling, archetype, and belief -- not features and pricing. Every other marketing document should be emotionally consistent with what is written here. If a landing page, email, or social post contradicts the tone, values, or emotional positioning described below, this document wins.

## Brand Archetype

### Primary: The Sage

The Sage seeks truth above all else. Not truth as a slogan -- truth as a practice. The Sage would rather show you an unflattering diagnostic than let you walk around believing you are further along than you are. The Sage respects knowledge too much to cheapen it with false encouragement.

This is the archetype that fits Mandarin because the entire product is built around the principle that honest data serves the learner better than comfortable fiction. The app does not tell you "Great job!" when you get 14 out of 22 correct. It tells you: 14/22. 63.6%. Your tones need work. Here is what you are drilling tomorrow.

**How the Sage manifests in the product:**

- Per-skill HSK diagnostics that show you where each ability actually stands, even when the imbalance is uncomfortable. Your vocabulary might be HSK 4 while your listening is HSK 2. The product will not blend those into a flattering average.
- Bayesian confidence dampening in the scheduling algorithm. A few good sessions do not inflate your score. Confidence is earned slowly, because that is how real competence develops.
- Projections with confidence intervals, not optimistic single dates. "HSK 4 by August at your current pace" is a grounded estimate, not a motivational poster.
- No XP, no gems, no hearts, no leaderboards. These are mechanisms for manufacturing a feeling of progress. The Sage does not manufacture feelings. The Sage shows you the data and trusts you to respond like an adult.

**What the Sage promises:** Understanding. Not the feeling of understanding -- actual understanding. The ability to look at your own learning and know, with evidence, where you stand and what to do next.

**What the Sage fears:** Delusion. The state where a learner believes they are HSK 4 because an app's green progress bar said so, but they cannot order food in a restaurant. The Sage fears the comfortable lie more than the uncomfortable truth, because the comfortable lie wastes months of a learner's life.

### Secondary: The Creator

The Creator builds things that did not exist before. Not for the sake of building, but because the thing that was needed was not available and nobody else was making it.

Mandarin was born from a real learning gap. Every existing tool was tried. None of them connected the experience of reading Chinese to the discipline of drilling vocabulary. So a text editor was opened and the building began. Not a startup -- a tool. For one learner's own use first, and then for anyone else who shared the frustration.

**How the Creator manifests in the brand:**

- The product is built by one person who uses it every day. This is not a corporate product designed by committee. It is a tool built by a learner for learners, and every design decision has been tested against real daily use.
- The build process is visible. The development blog covers what went wrong, what cognitive science research informed the design, and what is being built next. There is no mystification of the process. The thing is made of Python, SQLite, and care. You can see the seams.
- The aesthetic choices -- warm stone, bougainvillea rose, cypress olive, serif headings, unhurried Chinese characters -- reflect a builder who cares about craft, not just function. The Creator archetype does not ship ugly things just because they work. Beauty and usefulness are not in tension.

**What the Creator promises:** Something considered. Not a minimum viable product -- a carefully built tool where every drill type exists for a reason, every design choice has a justification, and the curriculum was thought through, not generated.

**What the Creator fears:** Mediocrity disguised as pragmatism. The "good enough" that is actually not good enough. The app that ships fast and iterates later but never actually gets good.

### What We Are NOT

**Not the Hero.** We do not save you. We do not rescue you from bad Chinese. You save yourself, through daily practice, honest self-assessment, and persistence. The app is a tool. You are the one who shows up every evening and does the work.

**Not the Magician.** We do not promise transformation. There is no moment where "everything clicks" and suddenly you speak Chinese. There is only the slow accumulation of correctly-recalled vocabulary, gradually-improving tone discrimination, and steadily-expanding reading ability. We will not pretend the work is anything other than work.

**Not the Jester.** Learning Chinese is not a game. It can be satisfying, even occasionally enjoyable, but it is not entertainment. We will not gamify the experience to make it feel fun at the expense of making it effective. The owl is not our mascot. We do not have a mascot.

**Not the Caregiver.** We are not going to hold your hand, soothe your frustration, or tell you everything is going to be okay. We respect you too much for that. If your tones are bad, we will show you the data. If you have not practiced in a week, we will not send a guilt notification -- we will just be here when you come back, without commentary, ready to continue.

## Brand Manifesto

Here is a truth that most language learning apps would rather you not think about: the feeling of learning and the fact of learning are often completely different things.

You can spend forty-five minutes matching pictures to words, tapping through multiple-choice questions, watching a progress bar fill to green, and leave a study session feeling accomplished. You can maintain a 200-day streak and earn enough gems to fill a digital warehouse. You can hit "Level 12" in an app and believe, with genuine sincerity, that you are making progress.

And then someone speaks to you in Chinese and you understand nothing.

This is not a failure of effort. It is a failure of design. The apps that dominate language learning were built to optimize for engagement -- time in app, daily opens, social shares, subscription renewals. And engagement, it turns out, is only loosely correlated with retention. You can be deeply engaged and learning almost nothing. You can be having a great time and building no lasting memory. The game mechanics work exactly as designed. They just were not designed for you.

There is a gap in Chinese learning that nobody talks about directly, probably because it is bad for marketing. The gap is between studying and knowing. Between the controlled environment of an app and the uncontrolled environment of real Chinese -- a WeChat message, a restaurant menu, a news headline, a conversation that moves faster than your recall. Studying happens in one place. Knowing happens in another. And for most learners, those two places are barely connected.

We built Mandarin to close that gap. Not by making studying feel more like real life -- that is the gamification approach and it does not work -- but by making real encounters feed directly into study. When you read Chinese in the app and look up a word you do not know, that word becomes practice. Not a flashcard -- actual multi-skill practice. Tone discrimination. Cloze deletion. Audio matching. Sentence construction. The word you encountered in context gets drilled across twenty-seven different exercise types, scheduled by an algorithm adapted from memory science research, at intervals calibrated to your personal forgetting curve.

This is not the only thing the app does, but it is the thing that matters most, because it addresses the structural problem that no amount of polish or gamification can solve: the disconnection between what you read and what you drill.

We built this for a specific person. Not a demographic -- a disposition. The person who has tried two or three Chinese learning tools and gotten frustrated with each one for different but related reasons. The person who suspects that their Duolingo streak is measuring dedication to the app, not progress in the language. The person who respects Anki but does not want to spend their study time managing decks. The person who wants to be told the truth about where they stand, even when the truth is that their listening is two levels behind their reading and they have been avoiding the hard work of fixing it.

This person does not want to be entertained. They want to be effective. They do not want encouragement. They want evidence. They do not want a learning journey. They want a learning system that works, that respects their time, that tells them the truth, and that does not waste a single minute of their evening on anything that does not produce real, measurable, lasting improvement.

We built Mandarin for that person. We know that person well, because the person who built it is that person.

The app is quiet. The metrics are real. The design assumes you are an adult. The sessions are short and focused, and when they end, there is no confetti and no celebration -- just an honest accounting of what you did and what comes next. It is not exciting. It is not addictive. It is not a game.

It is a tool. And it works.

## Origin Story (Brand Version)

### The 3-Minute Version

Mandarin began the way most useful tools begin: someone needed it and it did not exist.

The problem was simple and specific. Every Chinese learning tool separated two activities that should have been connected: reading Chinese and drilling vocabulary. You would study words in one place and encounter Chinese in another, and the gap between those two experiences was enormous. Nothing linked what you were studying to what you were encountering. Looking up a word during reading and drilling vocabulary were separate activities, happening in separate tools, with no feedback loop between them.

The search started where everyone's starts. Duolingo was fine for a week. The green owl kept you coming back, the multiple-choice questions were easy enough to feel productive, and the streak counter gave a daily sense of accomplishment. Then you tried to read a menu in Chinese and could not identify a single character you had supposedly learned. The progress bar had been measuring engagement with the app, not engagement with the language.

Anki, the spaced repetition tool that serious language learners swear by, was next. Anki is powerful. It is also, essentially, a blank engine. You have to build the car yourself. More time went into formatting flashcards -- Should there be audio? Pinyin on the front or back? Both recognition and production directions? -- than into studying Chinese. The tool was excellent at scheduling reviews. It offered nothing for content, curriculum, listening, tones, or the dozen other skills that make up actual Chinese ability.

HelloChinese, Hack Chinese, textbook courses -- each solved one piece. None solved the core problem: the disconnection between reading and drilling.

That gap became the product. A text editor was opened and the building began -- not a startup, not a side project, a tool that was actually needed. Python. SQLite. A spaced repetition scheduler adapted from FSRS, the open-source algorithm that represents the current state of memory science research. Drill types: not just flashcards, but tone pair discrimination, cloze deletion, audio-to-character matching, sentence construction, register-aware practice. Twenty-seven types in total, each targeting a specific cognitive skill, each grounded in research on how memory forms and retrieval strengthens.

And then the core feature, the one that solved the original problem: the cleanup loop. Read Chinese in the graded reader. Look up a word you do not know. That word automatically enters your drill queue, scheduled across multiple drill types. The thing you encountered during reading becomes the thing you practice during drilling. The loop closes. Reading and practice are no longer separate activities.

The diagnostics came next, because knowing where you actually stand matters -- not a single blended score, but per-skill tracking. Vocabulary at one level, listening at another, reading at a third, tones at a fourth. The imbalance was obvious once measured: reading ahead of listening by two full HSK levels. No other tool had ever surfaced this. Once the gap was visible, it became actionable.

Today, Mandarin is a complete adaptive drilling and reading system. Two hundred ninety-nine seed items across HSK 1-3. Twenty-seven drill types. Per-skill diagnostics with HSK projection. A graded reader with the cleanup loop. Speaking drills with tone grading. An aesthetic -- warm stone, bougainvillea rose, cypress olive -- designed to feel like sitting in a good library, not logging into a game.

Zero AI tokens at runtime. Every drill, every score, every recommendation is deterministic. The app works offline, responds instantly, and never generates content on the fly. Every sentence has been verified. Every number is defensible.

One person built this. The same person uses it every day. When something does not work, it gets noticed immediately -- because the builder is also the learner. The distance between builder and user is zero.

The philosophy is simple: honest metrics, no praise inflation, respect for the learner. The score is what it is. The projection is what the data supports. The app does not tell you that you are doing great. It tells you where you stand, and it trusts you to do the work.

### The 30-Second Version

Mandarin was born from a gap every serious Chinese learner hits: studying vocabulary and reading Chinese were separate experiences with no connection between them. Mandarin is an adaptive drilling and reading system where every word you look up during reading automatically becomes multi-skill practice. Twenty-seven drill types, honest per-skill diagnostics, spaced repetition scheduling from memory science research, and zero AI tokens at runtime. Built by one person who uses it every day for their own study. Free for HSK 1-2. Full access for twelve dollars a month.

### The One-Line Version

The Chinese study tool that did not exist, built by a learner who needed it -- where every word you look up becomes real practice.

## Brand Values

### 1. Honesty Over Comfort

**Definition:** Metrics tell the truth, even when it is discouraging.

**In the product:** When a user's diagnostic shows their listening at HSK 2 and their vocabulary at HSK 4, the app displays both numbers clearly, without softening or reframing. No "Your listening is almost there!" -- just the number. The bayesian confidence dampening means scores do not jump after a lucky session. They move slowly, reflecting real change, not statistical noise.

**In communication:** Blog posts and social content acknowledge what the product does not do well yet. The development blog covers past mistakes openly -- over-engineering the audio system, optimizing the algorithm before there was data, underestimating curriculum design. Marketing copy never claims the app is "the best" or "revolutionary." It describes what the app does and lets the reader evaluate.

**Violating this value:** Adding a "Great job!" message after a mediocre session. Displaying a single blended score that hides skill imbalances. Sending a push notification that says "You're so close to HSK 4!" when the data does not support it. Removing the decimal from an accuracy percentage to make it look cleaner.

### 2. Respect for Time

**Definition:** Every minute studying should produce real learning.

**In the product:** Sessions are built from drills the algorithm has determined are highest-value for the learner right now -- the items closest to their forgetting threshold, the skills most in need of work. There is no padding. No review of material already mastered just to fill time. No forced tutorials or unskippable intros. The interleaving enforcement mixes drill types within sessions because research shows this produces stronger retention, even though blocked practice feels more organized. The app optimizes for retention per minute, not for minutes in app.

**In communication:** Feature descriptions explain why each feature matters for learning, not just what it does. Pricing is stated directly -- twelve dollars a month, no annual upsell games, no "save 40% with annual billing" dark pattern. Blog posts are as long as they need to be and no longer. No fluff paragraphs that exist only for SEO word count.

**Violating this value:** Adding a social feed or leaderboard to increase time-in-app without increasing learning. Sending "come back!" notifications designed to generate opens rather than learning. Making the user click through three screens to start a session. Hiding the "skip" button on an onboarding tutorial.

### 3. Depth Over Breadth

**Definition:** Master Chinese deeply rather than dabble in fifty languages.

**In the product:** Mandarin does one language, with twenty-seven drill types, per-skill diagnostics, a graded reader with the cleanup loop, speaking drills with tone grading, and HSK-aligned curriculum through level 9. Every feature is designed specifically for the challenges of learning Chinese -- tone discrimination, character recognition, the gap between formal and colloquial register. This is not a generic language-learning platform with Chinese bolted on. The tone pair drills exist because Mandarin has tones. The register-aware drills exist because written and spoken Chinese diverge more than most languages. Every feature earns its place by addressing something specific about this language.

**In communication:** The product is always called "Mandarin," not "a language learning app." Copy refers to specific Chinese learning challenges -- tones, characters, HSK levels -- not generic "language learning" problems. Comparison pages explain exactly how the app handles Chinese-specific challenges that general tools miss.

**Violating this value:** Adding a second language. Building a "Spanish mode" because the market is bigger. Generalizing the UI so it works for any language, at the cost of losing the Chinese-specific features that make it effective. Marketing the app as "a language learning platform" to broaden the addressable market.

### 4. Earned Confidence

**Definition:** Confidence comes from evidence, not encouragement.

**In the product:** The HSK projection does not tell you "You can do it!" It tells you: "At your current pace, HSK 4 by August. Last month it said October." The confidence comes from watching the projection move, based on your demonstrated performance. The diagnostic does not soften the blow when a skill is lagging. It shows you the gap and then adjusts your practice to close it. When you improve, you know it is real because the numbers changed -- not because the app congratulated you.

**In communication:** Testimonials and case studies focus on specific, verifiable outcomes: "I went from HSK 2 to HSK 3 in four months" -- not "This app changed my life!" Social content shares real data points from actual learning sessions: tone accuracy percentages, session logs, specific plateau periods and what broke through them. No vague success stories.

**Violating this value:** Adding a "You're doing great!" message that triggers regardless of performance. Awarding badges or achievements for showing up rather than for demonstrated improvement. Celebrating streaks -- which measure consistency, not competence -- as if they were accomplishments. Posting testimonials that are emotional rather than evidential.

### 5. Quiet Craft

**Definition:** Build something beautiful and useful, without shouting about it.

**In the product:** The "Civic Sanctuary" aesthetic -- warm stone, bougainvillea rose, cypress olive, Cormorant Garamond headings, Noto Serif SC for Chinese characters -- exists because a tool you use every day should be a place you want to be, not just a place that works. The session-complete sound is a soft chime, not a victory fanfare. The dark mode respects the system preference automatically. The transitions are smooth but not showy. Nothing bounces, pulses, or demands attention. The craft is in what you do not notice: consistent spacing, careful typography, colors that are warm without being saccharine.

**In communication:** The app speaks for itself. Marketing does not rely on superlatives ("the best," "the most advanced," "revolutionary"). It describes what the app does and lets the reader decide. The blog posts are personal essays, not press releases. The social media presence is infrequent but substantive -- one thoughtful post is worth more than five content-calendar fillers. The brand does not chase trends, jump on memes, or adopt whatever voice is performing well on social this week.

**Violating this value:** Adding animation for the sake of animation. Using a neon color palette because it "pops." Posting daily on social media with thin content just to maintain algorithmic visibility. Writing a blog post titled "10 Reasons Mandarin Will Change How You Learn Chinese!" Using exclamation marks in feature descriptions.

## Emotional Design Principles

### Opening the app

Like sitting down in a good library. The room is warm. The light is golden. There is no sound except the kind of ambient silence that means focus is about to happen. The interface is there, waiting, without asking anything of you. No notifications badge, no "Welcome back!" banner, no "You missed 3 days!" guilt counter. Just the quiet signal: your tools are here, your data is here, you can begin whenever you are ready. The emotional register is calm competence -- the feeling of entering a space that was designed for exactly what you are about to do. A designer building a new screen should ask: does this feel like walking into a library, or logging into a platform? If it is the latter, strip it back.

### Starting a session

Like picking up a well-worn book from the nightstand. You know where you left off. There is no friction between the intention to study and the act of studying. One tap and you are in the first drill. The app has already decided what you are practicing and in what order, based on your data. You do not need to choose a deck, select a mode, or configure anything. The feeling is immediacy -- the distance between "I want to study" and "I am studying" should be as close to zero as the interface allows. A developer building a session-start flow should count the taps between opening the app and seeing the first drill. If it is more than two, something is wrong.

### Getting a drill wrong

Like a teacher who nods and says "try again." No red flash, no buzzer sound, no life lost, no heart depleted. The wrong answer is marked -- clearly, so there is no ambiguity -- but the emotional weight is neutral. This one needs more work. It will come back. The information is: you did not know this yet. That is not a failure. That is data. The system now knows something useful about your recall, and it will schedule this item accordingly. A designer handling an incorrect-answer state should aim for the emotional register of a pencil mark in the margin -- noticed, recorded, not punished. The color should be distinct but not alarming. The feedback should be instant but not startling.

### Getting a drill right

Like the small satisfaction of filling in a crossword clue you had to think about. A moment of quiet confirmation. Correct. The answer resolves, the next drill appears. There is no animation, no point counter incrementing, no encouraging message. The satisfaction is intrinsic -- you knew the answer, or you retrieved it successfully, and that fact is its own reward. The system records the correct recall and adjusts the scheduling interval. A developer implementing a correct-answer state should resist every impulse to celebrate. The celebration is the knowledge. Everything else is noise.

### Completing a session

Like setting down your tools after good work. The session summary appears: 18/22 correct, 12 minutes, 3 items flagged for extra review. There is a soft chime -- the sound of completion, not victory. The data is there if you want to look at it. If you do not, you can close the app. No "Share your results!" prompt. No "Come back tomorrow for your streak!" No call to action at all. Just the quiet statement: here is what you did. The emotional register is the same as checking a task off a list -- done, noted, moving on. A designer building the session-complete screen should imagine the user closing a notebook and placing a pen on the desk. That is the feeling.

### Seeing your progress

Like checking a trail map partway through a hike and realizing you have climbed higher than you thought. The ground gained is visible, measured, and real. You are not competing with anyone. There is no percentile rank, no leaderboard position, no comparison to other users. There is just your trajectory over time: where you were, where you are, where the data projects you will be. The feeling is orientation -- understanding your position in a larger landscape. A designer building a progress view should optimize for clarity and honesty. Every chart and number should answer the question "where do I stand?" without editorializing. If the progress is slow, the chart shows slow progress. If there is a plateau, the chart shows a plateau. The data is not styled to feel better than it is.

### Hitting a plateau

Like weather. It happens. The progress chart flattens out, and the app does not panic on your behalf. There is no "Don't give up!" message, because that message implies you should be worried, and you should not be. Plateaus are part of the landscape of language learning. The app offers what it can: here is what the data shows. Your accuracy on tone pairs has stalled at 72%. Here are the specific pairs you are confusing. The system has increased the frequency of tone discrimination drills in your sessions. The emotional register is matter-of-fact, the way a weather forecast is matter-of-fact. It is raining. Bring an umbrella. The rain is not a judgment on you. A designer encountering a plateau state in the data should present it with the same neutrality as any other state. No special messaging. No motivational interstitials. Just the data and, if applicable, what the system is doing about it.

### Reading a graded passage

Like reading a letter from a friend who writes simply so you can understand. The passage is at your level -- not too easy, not overwhelming. When you encounter an unfamiliar word, you tap it, and the meaning appears inline. No quiz popup, no "test yourself!" prompt. Just the answer to the question you had: what does this mean? The passage exists for you to enjoy and to learn from. Comprehension is the goal, not performance. The unknown words you looked up will appear in your drill queue later, but that happens in the background. Right now, you are just reading. A designer building the reading experience should optimize for immersion and calm. The glosses should appear and disappear without disrupting the reading flow. The typography should be generous and unhurried. The Chinese characters should be large enough to see stroke structure. The experience should feel like reading, not like taking a test.

### Looking up a word

Like asking a patient friend, "What does this mean?" The answer comes without judgment, without a quiz, without a popup asking if you want to add it to a deck. The definition is there. Pinyin, meaning, maybe an example. That is it. But behind the scenes, the word enters the drill queue, and the next time you practice, it will be there -- across multiple drill types, at intervals determined by the scheduling algorithm. The friend who told you what it meant will also, without being asked, quiz you on it later. That is the cleanup loop, and it should feel invisible. A developer implementing the lookup flow should ensure that the transition from "I don't know this word" to "now I do, for now" is as frictionless as a conversation. One tap. One answer. Back to reading.

## Brand Touchstone Moments

### 1. The First Honest Score

**What happens:** A new user completes their first diagnostic. The results come back: vocabulary HSK 2, listening HSK 1, tones 58% accuracy. The numbers are lower than they expected. They have been using another app for six months and believed they were further along.

**Why it matters:** This is the moment of trust. Every other app has been telling this person they are doing well. We are the first to tell them the truth. The initial feeling is deflation -- but what follows, if the design is right, is respect. The app did not try to make them feel good. It showed them where they stand. That is what a serious tool does.

**How we handle it:** The diagnostic results are presented clearly, without apology or softening, but also without harshness. The tone is factual: here are your scores across four skills. Here is what each number means. Here is what the system recommends focusing on first. No "Don't worry!" No "You'll get there!" Just: this is the starting point. Everything that follows builds from here.

**What the user feels:** A brief sting, followed by something rare in language learning apps: the sense that this tool is going to tell them the truth. That the numbers mean something. That when they improve, they will know it is real.

### 2. The Cleanup Loop Click

**What happens:** A user is reading a graded passage. They tap a word they do not know -- maybe 虽然 (*suiran*, although). The definition appears inline. They keep reading. The next day, during a drill session, 虽然 appears in a cloze deletion exercise. The user pauses. "Wait. That is the word I looked up yesterday."

**Why it matters:** This is the moment the user understands what makes this app different from everything else they have tried. Reading and drilling are not separate activities. The app noticed what they did not know and is now training them on it, automatically, across multiple drill types. The feedback loop is closed. It is the moment the product's core value proposition stops being a marketing claim and becomes a lived experience.

**How we handle it:** We do not call attention to it. There is no popup saying "This word came from your reading session!" The connection should feel natural, almost inevitable -- of course the app drills you on what you looked up. That is what a good system does. The elegance is in the seamlessness.

**What the user feels:** A small, satisfying click of recognition. The sense that the system is paying attention. That their reading and their practice are part of the same activity, not two separate chores. A quiet thought: "This is how it should work."

### 3. The Quiet Session

**What happens:** Tuesday evening. Twenty minutes between dinner and sleep. The user opens the app, completes a session: 18/22 correct, a few tone drills, some cloze exercises, two items from yesterday's reading. Session complete. They close the app. No notification follows. No email the next morning. No social share prompt.

**Why it matters:** This is the most common interaction with the product, and it is the one that defines the brand more than any feature or diagnostic. The quiet session is what daily practice actually looks like -- not a dramatic breakthrough, not a milestone celebration, just twenty minutes of work. The app's job is to make those twenty minutes as effective as possible and then get out of the way. The accumulation of hundreds of quiet sessions is what produces fluency. Not any single one of them.

**How we handle it:** The session ends with a summary, a soft chime, and nothing else. The data is recorded. The scheduling algorithm updates. The next session is already being prepared. The app does not ask the user to do anything, share anything, or feel anything. It simply records the work and waits for tomorrow.

**What the user feels:** The simple satisfaction of having done the thing they intended to do. Not excitement. Not pride. Just the calm knowledge that they studied, that the study was focused and efficient, and that tomorrow they will do it again. Over weeks and months, this feeling compounds into something more powerful than any gamification mechanic: a habit.

### 4. The Skill Imbalance Reveal

**What happens:** A user who has been studying for a few months opens their diagnostics. The per-skill breakdown shows: vocabulary HSK 3, reading HSK 3, listening HSK 2, tones 64%. They knew their listening felt weak, but they did not know it was a full level behind. And they had no idea their tones were lagging that much.

**Why it matters:** This is the moment the user understands why they have felt stuck. It was not a lack of effort. It was a lack of balance. They have been studying in a way that advances some skills and neglects others, and no tool had ever shown them the shape of their ability until now. The imbalance is the diagnosis. Once visible, it becomes actionable.

**How we handle it:** The per-skill breakdown is displayed clearly, with each skill's HSK-equivalent level. The system shows what it is doing in response: "Your sessions now include 35% listening drills, up from 20%, to address the gap." The tone is clinical, not alarming. An imbalance is normal -- it is the default state of every language learner. The value is in seeing it.

**What the user feels:** The particular satisfaction of a mystery solved. That vague sense of "I should be further along than this" now has a specific, addressable explanation. The frustration is redirected from "I'm not good enough" to "I've been training unevenly, and now I can fix that." It is the difference between a feeling and a fact, and the fact is infinitely more useful.

### 5. The Projection Shift

**What happens:** A user checks their HSK projection and sees: "HSK 4 by August. Last month: October." Two months shaved off the timeline, not because the algorithm became more generous, but because the user's demonstrated pace improved.

**Why it matters:** This is earned confidence made visible. The projection moved not because the app wanted to encourage the user, but because the data changed. The user showed up, did the work, and the math shifted in their favor. There is no "Congratulations!" message. The numbers simply moved. And because the user knows the numbers are honest -- because the app has never inflated anything -- they can trust that this improvement is real.

**How we handle it:** The projection is displayed with the previous month's estimate for comparison. The change is not highlighted or celebrated. It is just there, in the data, for the user to notice. If they want to dig into why -- which skills improved, which drill types contributed -- the data is available. But the headline is simple: the math moved. You are closer than you were.

**What the user feels:** A quiet glow. Not the spike of a badge or achievement, but the deeper satisfaction of watching something you have been working on, slowly, daily, actually change. The feeling is not "I'm great" but "this is working." That distinction matters. One is about the person. The other is about the process. We care about the process.

## Brand Language Patterns

### Naming Conventions

**Feature names:** Plain English verbs and actions. "Cleanup Loop." "Graded Reader." "Tone Pairs." "Session Summary." If a feature name could be a chapter heading in a straightforward textbook, it is probably right. If it sounds like a trademarked product name, it is wrong. The word "Pro" never appears. The word "Smart" never modifies a noun. Nothing is "powered by" anything.

**HSK levels:** Always "HSK 3," never "Level 3," "Stage 3," or "Tier 3." HSK is the internationally recognized framework for Chinese proficiency. We use it because our learners use it, and because it is a real, external standard -- not a proprietary system we invented.

**Drill types:** Descriptive names that say what the drill does. "Tone pair discrimination." "Cloze deletion." "Audio-to-hanzi matching." "Sentence construction." Not "ToneMaster." Not "FillPro." Not "SoundMatch." If a drill type name requires explanation, the name is wrong.

**Session outcomes:** Factual. "Session complete. 18/22 correct. 12 minutes." Not "Amazing session!" Not "Almost perfect!" Not "Keep it up!" The numbers speak for themselves. Editorializing adds nothing and undermines the trust we are building with honest data.

**Error states:** Specific and helpful. "That drill didn't load -- try refreshing." Not "Oops! Something went wrong." Not "Uh oh!" Not anything that treats an error as an opportunity to be cute.

### Sentence Patterns We Use

- "Here's where you stand: [data]."
- "[Feature] does [thing]. Here's why that matters."
- "You [did X]. [What that means for your learning]."
- "At your current pace, [projection]."
- "The honest answer is [honest answer]."
- "Your [skill] is [level]. Your [other skill] is [other level]. Now you know where to focus."
- "[Number] drill types. [Number] items reviewed. [Number] minutes."
- "This is what the data shows."

### Sentence Patterns We Avoid

- "Congratulations! You [trivial action]!"
- "Don't miss out on [thing]!"
- "You're doing great!" (unless the data objectively supports it)
- "Unlock [thing] by [action]!"
- "Join X thousand learners who [claim]!"
- "Don't give up!"
- "You're almost there!"
- "Amazing work!"
- "Your learning journey [anything]."
- "Science-backed" (too vague -- cite the specific science or say nothing).
- "AI-powered" (we use zero AI tokens at runtime; this phrase would be dishonest and, in the broader market, has become meaningless).

## Brand Mood Board (Text Description)

### Colors

Warm stone walls in a Kyoto temple in late afternoon. The grey is not grey -- it is stone-colored, which means it has warmth in it, a memory of the earth it was cut from. Rose: not the bright pink of a notification badge, but the muted, sun-faded bougainvillea of a Mediterranean courtyard wall, the color that says "someone built this by hand and the light has been settling on it for decades." Olive: not the vivid green of foliage, but the grey-green of old cypress, steady and unshowy -- the green of things that have been growing for a long time. Paper: warm cream, the color of a page in a book that was printed well and has aged even better. Never blue-white. Blue-white is the color of screens, not of things worth reading.

### Textures

Handmade paper grain -- the kind you can feel with your fingertips, where the fibers are visible and the surface is not perfectly smooth. Worn wood: a desk surface where someone has been working for years, where the grain shows through because the varnish has worn away in the places where hands rest. Linen cloth: the cover of a good notebook, or the fabric of a shade that softens direct light. Nothing glossy. Nothing chrome. Nothing glass. Glossy surfaces reflect the world outside the screen. We want the user's attention inside.

### Spaces

A library reading room with tall windows. Not a modern library with fluorescent lighting and plastic chairs -- an older library, with wood and fabric and the kind of silence that is actually quiet, not the performative silence of a coworking space. A Japanese study with tatami and a low desk, where the floor is warm and the only objects are the ones you need. A cafe in Taipei with warm light and no music, where the drink in front of you is there to keep your hands busy while your mind is elsewhere. A university corridor at dusk, empty but inviting -- the classes are over, the students have gone, and the building itself has settled into a quiet that feels like an invitation to stay and work.

### Light

Golden hour, always. The light in the app -- the colors, the contrast, the warmth -- should feel like the hour before sunset, when the light is low and warm and makes everything look better than it does at noon. Never fluorescent. Fluorescent light is the light of obligation, of offices and waiting rooms. Never noon harsh -- that is the light of exposure, of seeing too clearly, of being scrutinized. The light in Mandarin is the light of late afternoon: warm, forgiving, focused. Shadows exist but they are soft, not dramatic. They suggest depth without creating tension.

### Type

Serif headings like letterpress imprints -- Cormorant Garamond, with its tall ascenders and elegant contrast, suggesting something that was set in type deliberately, not generated by a system. The letters look like someone chose them. Clean serif body text -- Source Serif 4, readable and unpretentious, like a well-typeset book that does not call attention to its typography because the typography is so good it disappears. Chinese characters in Noto Serif SC, generous and unhurried -- large enough to see stroke structure, spaced comfortably, given the room they deserve. Chinese characters are architecture. They should be displayed like architecture: with space around them, with respect for their complexity, at a size that honors the centuries of refinement that produced them.

### Sound

The session-complete chime is soft, brief, and tonal -- like a single note on a glass bowl, or the sound of a small bell in a garden. It signals completion, not victory. The session-start sound, if it exists at all, is barely there -- a breath, an arrival, the auditory equivalent of a door opening quietly. No fanfare. No synthetic reward sounds. No slot-machine dings. The silence between sounds matters more than the sounds themselves. Most of the time, the app is silent, and that silence is part of the design.

## Closing Note

This document defines who the brand is, not what the product does. The product will change -- new features, new content, new drill types. The brand should not change, because the brand is a set of beliefs about how learning should feel and how a tool should treat the person using it.

If a future feature or communication feels inconsistent with this document, the question is not "should we update the brand narrative?" The question is "should we ship this feature or send this communication?" The brand is the filter. Everything passes through it.

The emotional identity of Mandarin can be stated simply: we built an honest tool for serious learners, and we believe that honesty, quiet craft, and respect for the learner's time and intelligence are not just design choices but moral commitments. We would rather have a small number of users who trust the product completely than a large number of users who feel vaguely good about an app that is secretly wasting their time.

That is the brand. Everything else follows from it.
