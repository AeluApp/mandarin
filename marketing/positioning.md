## Target Audiences (ranked by priority)

### Primary: Self-study adults (25-45) actively working through HSK levels

**Demographics:** Ages 25-45, college-educated, professional or graduate student, disposable income for a $14.99/month subscription without blinking. Skews male 60/40 but the gap is closing. Located primarily in English-speaking countries (US, UK, Canada, Australia) with a secondary cluster of expats living in China, Taiwan, or Singapore.

**Psychographics:** They treat language learning as a serious project, not a casual hobby. They research tools before committing. They have read at least one Reddit thread comparing Anki decks. They value efficiency over entertainment — they would rather spend 20 focused minutes than 45 distracted ones. They are skeptical of gamification because they have been burned by Duolingo-style apps that felt productive but produced no lasting retention. They respect data and want to see exactly where they stand, even when the news is bad.

**Current tools:** Most are using 2-4 tools simultaneously: Anki or Hack Chinese for SRS, Pleco as a dictionary, a textbook or course (HSK Standard Course, Chinese Zero to Hero, Mandarin Blueprint), and possibly HelloChinese or Du Chinese for reading. They feel the friction of context-switching between these tools daily. Some use Notion or spreadsheets to track their own progress because no single tool gives them the full picture.

**Frustrations:** "I study vocabulary but can't read real Chinese." "I don't know if I'm actually HSK 3 or just think I am." "I spend more time managing my Anki decks than studying." "Duolingo tells me I'm doing great but I can't order food in Mandarin." "My listening is way behind my reading and nothing is fixing that."

**Goals:** Pass HSK 3-5 within 1-2 years. Be able to read Chinese web content, watch Chinese media with less subtitle dependence, and hold basic-to-intermediate conversations. They want measurable, honest progress they can point to.

**Learning context:** Evening study sessions (20-40 minutes) after work or kids are in bed. Some do a shorter session on their commute (mobile browser). Weekends might allow a longer 45-60 minute session. Consistency matters more to them than marathon sessions, and they know it.

### Secondary: HSK exam preppers

**Demographics:** Overlaps with primary audience but with a hard deadline. University students needing HSK 4 for a program requirement. Professionals needing HSK 5 for a job in China. Self-studiers who set an exam date to force accountability.

**Timeline pressure:** 2-6 months until their exam. They are searching for "HSK [level] practice" and "am I ready for HSK [level]" right now.

**Specific needs:** Skill-level diagnostics that tell them where they are across vocabulary, listening, reading, and tones — not a single blunt score. Gap identification: "Your vocabulary is HSK 4 but your listening is HSK 3 — focus there." Projection: "At your current pace, you'll be HSK 4-ready by [date]." The new HSK 3.0 (July 2026) adds a mandatory speaking component, and they are anxious about it.

**What they are searching for:** "HSK 4 practice test," "HSK readiness check," "how to know if I'm ready for HSK," "HSK 3.0 speaking requirement," "HSK study plan [X] months."

**Switching trigger:** They find us when their current tool cannot tell them whether they are ready. Anki can't. Duolingo can't. We can.

### Tertiary: Anki power users hitting friction

**What they love about Anki:** Total control. Open-source ethos. The SRS engine is proven. Huge community deck library. Free.

**What frustrates them:** Deck management is a second job. Card formatting is inconsistent across community decks. No built-in audio, listening, or speaking practice. No diagnostics beyond "cards due." Anki tells you nothing about your skill balance — you could be great at recognition and terrible at production and never know. The learning curve for new users is brutal, and even experienced users spend time on settings and plugins that should be spent studying.

**What would make them switch:** An app that keeps the SRS rigor (we use FSRS, the same algorithm family) but removes the deck-management overhead, adds multiple drill types beyond flashcards, and provides skill-level diagnostics. They will not switch to anything that feels dumbed down. They need to see the algorithm is serious. "Adaptive spaced repetition with confidence-weighted scheduling" is a selling point for this audience specifically.

**Switching trigger:** They see the 44 drill types, recognize the FSRS lineage, and realize they can get Anki-quality scheduling without building their own curriculum.

### Tertiary: Heritage speakers filling gaps

**What they already have:** Conversational fluency (often regional dialect-influenced). Listening comprehension in family/social contexts. Cultural familiarity. Tone production is generally native-level in speech.

**What they lack:** Character recognition and reading ability. Formal/written register. Grammar terminology and explicit pattern awareness. HSK-aligned vocabulary beyond conversational basics. Writing ability. They can say it but they cannot read it, and they feel embarrassed about the gap.

**What they need from us:** A way to skip ahead on listening and speaking drills (they do not need tone pair practice) and focus on reading, character recognition, and formal grammar. Our per-skill diagnostics let them see their lopsided profile and attack the specific gaps. The graded reader is especially valuable — they can comprehend the meaning but are building character recognition.

**Switching trigger:** They try a general Chinese app and are forced to sit through "Ni hao" for three weeks. We let them test in at their actual level per skill.

### Anti-audience: Who is NOT for us

Be explicit in all messaging. We are not for:

- **Casual tourists** wanting 50 phrases for a trip. They should use Google Translate or a phrasebook. We are a training system, not a translation tool.
- **Children under 16.** Our interface is designed for adults. No cartoon mascots, no reward animations, no parental controls. A motivated teenager could use it, but we do not design for or market to children.
- **People who primarily want conversation practice.** We do not have a chat partner, AI or human. For conversation, use HelloTalk, Tandem, or iTalki. We build the underlying skills (vocabulary, grammar, listening, tones) that make conversations productive, but we are not a conversation tool.
- **People who want a human tutor experience.** We are a drill and diagnostic system. If they want someone to explain grammar, correct essays, or have unstructured conversation, iTalki is the right tool. We complement tutoring; we do not replace it.
- **People who are not willing to do daily practice.** Spaced repetition requires consistency. If someone will use the app once a week, the algorithm cannot function properly and they will not see results. We should be honest about this in onboarding.

## Core Value Proposition

**Every word you look up becomes practice — and every practice session targets exactly what you don't know.**

Supporting statements:

1. **The cleanup loop closes the study-to-reality gap.** When you read Chinese and tap a word you don't recognize, that word enters your drill queue automatically. You are never drilling random vocabulary — you are drilling the specific words you actually encountered and didn't know.

2. **44 drill types train the skill you're weakest at, not just the skill that's easiest to test.** Recognition, production, listening discrimination, tone accuracy, cloze comprehension, register awareness, sentence construction. The system knows which skills lag and shifts your practice mix accordingly.

3. **Honest diagnostics tell you where you actually stand.** Per-skill HSK projection, multi-criteria readiness assessment, and specific weakness identification. No green progress bars that make you feel good while hiding real gaps.

## Brand Pillars

### 1. Honest Metrics

**Definition:** Every number in the app is real, defensible, and actionable. We never inflate progress, manufacture encouragement, or hide bad news behind positive framing.

**Proof points:**
- Per-skill HSK readiness scores (vocabulary, listening, reading, tones tracked independently)
- HSK projection with confidence intervals, not a single optimistic date
- No streak anxiety mechanics — streaks are tracked but never weaponized
- confidence-weighted scheduling means scores stabilize slowly; a few good sessions don't create false confidence

**How it shows up in copy:**
- "Your listening is HSK 2. Your reading is HSK 4. Now you know where to focus."
- "Honest progress, not encouraging lies."
- Never use: "Great job!", "You're doing amazing!", "Almost there!"

### 2. Adaptive Depth

**Definition:** The system adjusts not just timing (when to review) but type (how to review) and focus (what to prioritize) — all deterministically, without AI token calls, based on your demonstrated performance across multiple skill dimensions.

**Proof points:**
- Adaptive spaced repetition with confidence-weighted scheduling schedules review timing
- Interleaving enforcement mixes drill types within sessions for stronger retention
- Day profile adaptation adjusts session composition based on time of day and available duration
- The system narrows focus to weak areas automatically — if your tones are lagging, you get more tone drills

**How it shows up in copy:**
- "The system knows your tones are behind your vocabulary. Tomorrow's session adjusts."
- "44 drill types, and the system picks the right one."
- Never use: "AI-powered", "smart algorithm" (too vague), "personalized journey"

### 3. The Closed Loop

**Definition:** Reading and drilling are not separate activities. What you read feeds what you drill. What you drill improves what you can read. The cycle is automatic and continuous.

**Proof points:**
- The cleanup loop: words looked up in graded reading become SRS drill items
- Graded reader with inline glosses matched to your HSK level
- Listening passages with transcript reveal — words you miss feed back into drills
- No manual card creation, no deck management, no import/export friction

**How it shows up in copy:**
- "Every word you look up becomes practice."
- "Read. Look up. Drill. Read better. Repeat."
- Never use: "all-in-one" (overused, means nothing), "ecosystem"

### 4. Respect for Adults

**Definition:** The product treats learners as capable adults who want efficiency and honesty, not gamification and praise. The interface is calm, the metrics are real, and the design assumes intelligence.

**Proof points:**
- No XP, hearts, leaderboards, or cartoon mascots
- No streak-shame notifications ("You're about to lose your streak!")
- "Civic Sanctuary" aesthetic: warm stone, teal, terracotta — designed to feel like a library, not an arcade
- Calm dark mode that respects prefers-color-scheme
- Session-complete sound is a soft chime, not a victory fanfare

**How it shows up in copy:**
- "Built for adults who want to learn, not play a game."
- "No owl. No guilt. No confetti."
- Never use: "fun way to learn", "make learning a game", "addictive"

## Category Positioning

### vs. Flashcard apps (Anki, Hack Chinese)

**What they do that we don't:** Anki offers unlimited customization — custom card templates, community-shared decks, plugins for every edge case. Hack Chinese has a massive HSK-aligned word list and clean UI for pure vocabulary drilling. Both let users build or import exactly the content they want.

**What we do that they don't:** 44 drill types vs. 1 (flashcard). Graded reading with the cleanup loop. Per-skill diagnostics across vocabulary, listening, reading, and tones. Speaking drills with tone grading. Interleaving enforcement. No deck management required — the curriculum is built in.

**When someone should use both:** A learner who has invested heavily in custom Anki decks (especially for specialized vocabulary beyond HSK 6) might keep Anki for that niche content while using Aelu for structured HSK progression, reading practice, and listening/speaking drills.

**The switching trigger:** "I'm spending more time managing my Anki decks than studying." or "I want listening and speaking practice integrated with my SRS, not in a separate app."

### vs. Gamified apps (Duolingo, HelloChinese)

**What they do that we don't:** Duolingo has massive brand recognition, social features, and a free tier that covers a lot of content. HelloChinese has decent handwriting practice and a polished beginner experience. Both are excellent at getting complete beginners to start — the onboarding friction is near zero.

**What we do that they don't:** 44 drill types vs. 3-6. Honest diagnostics instead of XP and streaks. Graded reading with cleanup loop. FSRS-based scheduling instead of basic intervals. Per-skill tracking. No engagement-hack mechanics (hearts, gems, leaderboards). Speaking drills with actual tone grading. Content designed for adults, not for the broadest possible audience.

**When someone should use both:** A beginner might start with HelloChinese for the first two weeks to get oriented with tones, pinyin, and basic stroke order, then switch to Aelu for serious structured study. There is no good reason to use Duolingo and Aelu simultaneously — they serve the same function at different quality levels.

**The switching trigger:** "I've been using Duolingo for six months and I still can't read a menu." or "I realized I'm optimizing for my streak, not for learning."

### vs. Course platforms (Chinese Zero to Hero, Mandarin Blueprint)

**What they do that we don't:** Structured video lessons with teacher explanations. Grammar instruction with examples and context. Community forums and cohort-based pacing. Human-presented content with personality and narrative. Mandarin Blueprint in particular has a strong system for character decomposition and mnemonics.

**What we do that they don't:** Adaptive SRS drilling that responds to individual performance. The cleanup loop. 44 drill types for active recall, not passive video watching. Per-skill diagnostics. Listening practice with speed control. Speaking drills. Offline access. The courses teach; we train. The distinction matters.

**When someone should use both:** This is a strong pairing. Use a course for instruction (understanding grammar, learning character logic, getting cultural context) and Aelu for practice (drilling, reading, listening, diagnostics). We are the gym; they are the coach.

**The switching trigger:** "I've watched all the videos but I can't recall anything without the textbook in front of me." or "I need active practice, not more passive input."

### vs. Dictionary/reference tools (Pleco, Written Chinese)

**What they do that we don't:** Pleco is the gold standard Chinese dictionary — comprehensive definitions, example sentences, OCR lookup, handwriting input, and deep reference content. Written Chinese offers character decomposition and stroke order. These are lookup tools optimized for the moment you encounter an unknown word.

**What we do that they don't:** Everything after the lookup. Pleco tells you what a word means right now. We tell you what it means, drill you on it later, track whether you've retained it, and integrate it into your practice across 44 drill types. Pleco is a dictionary. We are a training system.

**When someone should use both:** Pleco remains useful for looking up words outside the app — in real conversations, on signs, in WeChat messages. If a learner is reading Chinese outside of our graded reader, Pleco is the right lookup tool. Ideally, we will eventually offer a way to import Pleco lookup history into the drill queue, but that is future work.

**The switching trigger:** There is no switching trigger — these are complementary tools. But a Pleco user who realizes they keep looking up the same words and never retaining them is our exact target.

### vs. Language exchange (HelloTalk, Tandem)

**What they do that we don't:** Real conversations with native speakers. Cultural exchange. Correction from humans. The social motivation of having a language partner. Exposure to colloquial, natural Chinese that no textbook covers.

**What we do that they don't:** Structured skill building. Spaced repetition. Systematic vocabulary progression aligned to HSK. Diagnostics. Tone drilling. Everything that makes those conversations more productive. Language exchange exposes you to Chinese; we build the underlying skills that let you understand and produce it.

**When someone should use both:** Always. Language exchange and structured drilling are complementary, not competitive. We make the exchange sessions more productive by building vocabulary and listening skills. The exchange sessions expose gaps that feed back into our drill queue through the cleanup loop (if the learner reads in the app afterward).

**The switching trigger:** No switching trigger. These serve different functions entirely. A learner who is only using HelloTalk and plateauing needs structured practice — that is our entry point.

### vs. Tutoring services (iTalki, Preply)

**What they do that we don't:** Personalized instruction from a human expert. Real-time error correction. Conversation practice. Essay correction. Cultural explanation. The ability to ask "why?" and get a nuanced answer. Emotional support and accountability.

**What we do that they don't:** Cost efficiency ($14.99/month vs. $15-40/hour for tutoring). Available 24/7, not limited to scheduled sessions. Spaced repetition scheduling that a human tutor cannot replicate manually. 44 drill types in a single session. Per-skill diagnostics. Consistent, tireless, perfectly-remembering practice.

**When someone should use both:** This is the ideal combination for serious learners. Use a tutor for instruction, conversation, and questions. Use Aelu for daily drilling, reading, and diagnostic tracking. The tutor provides the understanding; we provide the repetition that converts understanding into automatic recall.

**The switching trigger:** No direct switching trigger — we complement tutors, not replace them. But a learner spending $200/month on tutoring who realizes half the session is vocabulary review might shift that review to us and use tutoring time for conversation and instruction.

## Voice & Tone Guidelines

### Brand voice

**Direct.** We say what we mean in as few words as possible. No hedging, no corporate softening, no "we believe that perhaps" constructions. "Your listening is behind your reading" — not "You might want to consider focusing a bit more on listening skills."

**Grounded.** Every claim is backed by a specific product feature, a research finding, or a data point. We do not make vague promises. "44 drill types" is grounded. "Revolutionary learning experience" is not.

**Calm.** We do not create urgency, anxiety, or FOMO. We do not yell in all caps. We do not use exclamation marks in feature descriptions. The tone is a knowledgeable peer explaining something clearly over coffee — not a salesperson, not a motivational speaker, not a professor.

**Respectful.** We assume the reader is intelligent and has done their research. We do not explain things they already know. We do not talk down. We do not over-explain. If they are reading our comparison page, they already know what Anki is — we do not need to define it.

### Tone shifts by context

**Landing page:** Confident and concise. Lead with the strongest claim. No fluff above the fold. Every sentence earns its space. Tone of someone who built something good and knows it, without arrogance.

**Error messages:** Helpful and specific. "That drill didn't load — try refreshing. If it happens again, email hello@aelu.app." Never blame the user. Never be cute. Never use "Oops!"

**Feature announcements:** Factual and brief. "New: listening drills now have adjustable playback speed (0.5x to 1.5x). Slower speed helps with tone discrimination at early levels." Explain the feature and why it matters. No hype.

**Social media:** Slightly warmer than the landing page. Can include personal observations from the team's learning experience. Still grounded — share specific data points, real struggles, concrete findings. Never performatively casual. Never use emoji as punctuation.

**Partner communications:** Professional and grateful without being effusive. Clear on logistics (commission rates, payout schedules). Honest about what the app does and doesn't do. Give partners everything they need to make their own assessment.

### Why $14.99/month

**Market context:**

|-----|-------|-------------|

| HelloChinese | $10/month | Full content access |
| Hack Chinese | $15/month | Full SRS vocabulary |
| Aelu | $14.99/month | 44 drill types, graded reader, listening, speaking, diagnostics |
| iTalki tutor | $15-40/hour | One hour of human instruction |

$14.99/month positions us alongside Hack Chinese and Skritter (which only do flashcards or handwriting). It signals seriousness and reflects the breadth of what we offer — 44 drill types, graded reading, listening, speaking, and diagnostics in one system.

**Value framing:**
- $14.99/month = $0.50/day. Less than a coffee. Less than a single bus fare.
- $14.99/month = $0.56 per drill type. 44 drill types, each targeting a different cognitive skill.
- $14.99/month vs. $30-120/month for weekly tutoring. We do not replace a tutor, but we handle 80% of what tutors spend session time on (vocabulary review, listening practice, tone drilling).
- $14.99/month for unlimited daily sessions. No per-session fees. No credit system.

**Why not freemium-heavy:**
The free tier is HSK 1-2 content — not a time-limited trial but a permanent, fully functional tier. This is deliberate:
- HSK 1-2 is where beginners evaluate whether the approach works for them. They need enough content to form a real judgment, not a 7-day teaser.
- HSK 1-2 is roughly 300 words and the foundational grammar. It is genuinely useful on its own, not a crippled demo.
- The paywall at HSK 3 is where learners have committed to Mandarin seriously. They have invested weeks or months. They know whether the app works for them. The conversion ask comes at maximum demonstrated value.
- Giving away HSK 1-2 forever means partners and reviewers can honestly say "try it free, no time limit" — which is a stronger recommendation than "it has a 7-day trial."

### Price objection responses

**"That's more than Duolingo."**
Duolingo Plus removes ads and adds offline access. The free version of Duolingo is what most people use, and it tests roughly 3 exercise types — all recognition-based. We offer 44 drill types including production, listening, speaking, and tone discrimination, plus graded reading with the cleanup loop, plus per-skill diagnostics. If Duolingo's approach is working for you, stay with it. If you've hit a plateau and suspect you're optimizing for streaks rather than retention, we're built for that exact problem.

**"Anki is free."**
Anki is genuinely excellent and free. If you enjoy building and maintaining decks, and you only need flashcard-style drills, Anki is hard to beat. We use the same algorithm family (FSRS). What we add: 26 additional drill types beyond flashcards, a graded reader with the cleanup loop, listening and speaking practice, per-skill diagnostics, and a built-in HSK-aligned curriculum so you never have to build a deck. The $14.99/month pays for not being a curriculum designer on top of being a learner.

**"I can find free resources."**
You absolutely can. Pleco is free. Anki is free. YouTube has thousands of hours of Chinese instruction. The challenge is not finding resources — it is combining them into a system that tracks your progress across skills, adapts to your weaknesses, and connects your reading experience to your drill practice. That integration is what you are paying for. If you have the discipline and time to build that system yourself from free tools, more power to you.

**"HelloChinese is cheaper."**
HelloChinese is $10/month — $4.99 less. They have good beginner content and a polished mobile experience. If you are a beginner who wants a gentle, guided introduction to Mandarin, HelloChinese is a reasonable choice. Our differences: 27 vs. ~6 drill types, the cleanup loop, FSRS-based scheduling, per-skill diagnostics with HSK projection, and speaking drills with tone grading. At HSK 3 and above, the depth gap becomes significant. The $2/month difference buys a meaningfully different training system.

Aelu is the only Chinese learning app that:

1. **Automatically turns every word you look up during reading into a multi-type drill** — not just a flashcard, but tone practice, cloze deletion, audio matching, sentence construction, and more. The cleanup loop is our single most defensible feature.

5. **Projects your HSK readiness date with multi-criteria confidence intervals** — not a single optimistic number, but a grounded estimate based on your demonstrated pace across all tracked skills.

6. **Enforces interleaving across drill types within a single session** — mixing recognition, production, and listening drills together, based on research showing interleaved practice produces stronger retention than blocked practice.

7. **Was built by a small team that uses the app daily for their own Mandarin study** — every design decision is tested against real daily use, not focus groups.

10. **"Chinese learning without the lies."**
Provocative. Implies competitors inflate progress (they do). Risks seeming negative or combative. Could work for a specific ad campaign but not as the permanent tagline.

**Recommendation:** Adopt #1 ("Every word you look up becomes practice.") as the primary tagline. Use #3 ("Honest Chinese practice for serious learners.") as the secondary/descriptor tagline for contexts where the cleanup loop reference needs a broader frame.
