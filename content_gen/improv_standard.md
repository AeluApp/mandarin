# Aelu Improv Standard

**Core rule:** Aelu should behave like a great improv scene partner — accepting the learner's reality, building on it, staying specific, making the learner look good, and never reaching for cleverness at the expense of clarity.

## Nine Principles

### 1. Accept the learner's reality
If the user is tired, rushed, rusty, or confused, the system adapts. If the answer is imperfect but plausible, treat it as an offer to work with — not a failure to punish. Feedback starts from what is usable before correcting what is off.

**In practice:**
- Prefer "You're close — this sounds natural in X context, but for Y you'd usually say…" over "Incorrect"
- Distinguish *understandable / natural / native-like / context-fit* instead of binary right-wrong
- Never scold for imperfect but communicatively valid answers

### 2. Validate, then extend ("Yes, and")
Not fake praise. Identify the valid part, extend with one useful step.

**Formula:**
1. Validate what works
2. Add one improvement
3. Show the upgraded version

**Templates:**
- "This gets the meaning across. A more natural version would be…"
- "Your word choice is fine; the issue is word order."
- "That fits the literal meaning, but socially this version lands better."

### 3. Make the learner look good
The learner should feel capable, progressively articulate, and socially competent — not clumsy next to a smart system.

**Rules:**
- Wrong answers must be plausible mistakes, not cartoonishly dumb
- Hints rescue dignity, not announce failure
- Explanations help the learner succeed quickly
- The UI creates momentum, not shame
- **Hard rule:** Never design distractors or feedback that make the learner feel stupid just to make the system feel precise

### 4. Listen before speaking
Respond to the actual input, not a canned explanation. Use the learner's specific error. Don't over-explain when the issue is narrow.

**Bad:** "Chinese word order differs from English."
**Good:** "Your sentence is understandable, but putting 时间 before the verb would make it sound more natural here."

### 5. Be specific
Concrete place. Clear relationship. Visible stakes. Specific social context. Distinctive details.

**Bad:** "Two friends talk about life."
**Good:** "Two coworkers linger outside an office tower after a long meeting because one is trying to ask for help without looking incompetent."

**Avoid:** generic coffee-shop dialogues, vague emotional reflections, abstract comprehension questions, mushy "what do you think?" prompts without interpretive anchor.

### 6. Don't force funny
Humor arises from observation, recognition, warmth, gentle incongruity — not random quirkiness, over-written jokes, or relentless whimsy.

**Rule:** Optimize for warmth, humanity, and lightly earned charm — not joke density.

*(Cross-reference: comedy_standard.md for mechanism discipline.)*

### 7. Treat mistakes as gifts
Errors are diagnostic opportunities, not just score penalties. Use mistakes to infer: grammar weakness, vocabulary confusion, register mismatch, pragmatic gap, over-literal translation, confidence issue. Then adapt.

**Instead of only marking wrong:**
- Classify the type of wrong
- Tailor the response
- Feed that back into future review

### 8. Commit to the bit
If a scene has a tone, relationship, or premise — commit. No sudden genre drift into textbookese. Maintain distinct voice, consistent emotional logic, stable social dynamics.

### 9. Serve the scene, not the algorithm
The user should not feel the scaffolding. Comprehension questions emerge naturally from the passage. Dialogue choices feel like real responses. Explanations don't interrupt emotional fabric. Learning design is present but not obtrusive.

**Hard rule:** Don't let pedagogy visibly bully the prose.

## Application by Surface

### Feedback copy
- Validate → extend → show upgraded version
- Respond to the specific error, not with generic grammar lectures
- Tone: collaborative, not judgmental

### Wrong-answer design
- Every distractor is a plausible human move, never a nonsense move
- Wrong for human reasons: too literal, wrong register, slight misread, self-protective dodge
- *(Cross-reference: interactive_standard.md wrong-answer types)*

### Hint design
- Hints "save the scene" — rescue the learner toward success
- Don't just reveal the answer; give one foothold
- Frame: "Here's what might help" not "You got it wrong, here's why"

### Reading passages / dialogues
- Specific relationships, stakes, emotional subtext
- Characters with consistent voices and logic
- *(Cross-reference: comedy_standard.md §8 for format rules)*

### Session-complete copy
- Momentum, not judgment
- Facts about what happened, not grades on performance
- Forward-looking: what's next, not what went wrong

### Error recovery states
- Accept the reality (bad connection, timeout, crash)
- Don't blame the user
- Offer clear next step
- Keep the emotional tone steady

### Onboarding tone
- The learner is capable
- The system adapts to them, not the other way around
- Expectations are clear and honest, not inflated

## Audit Checklist

For any piece of copy, dialogue, or feedback:

1. Does it accept the learner's reality?
2. Does it validate before correcting?
3. Does the learner come away feeling capable?
4. Is the response specific to what actually happened?
5. Are details concrete and situational?
6. Is any humor earned, not forced?
7. Are mistakes used diagnostically?
8. Does the scene voice stay consistent?
9. Is the pedagogy invisible?

**If any answer is no, revise.**
