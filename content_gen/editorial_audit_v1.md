# Editorial Audit V1 — Content Quality Pass

## EDITORIAL MEMO: The Seven Floor-Lowering Habits

After reading across all HSK levels — passages, dialogues, feedback lines, translations, and questions — here are the patterns most consistently dragging quality below A-level.

### 1. THE EXPLAINED MORAL

The single most common problem. A passage builds beautifully to a scene or image, then appends 1-2 sentences explaining what it means.

Frequency: ~40% of HSK 7-9 passages. Present but less damaging at HSK 4-6.

Pattern:
```
[strong scene or image]
我忽然明白，仪式的意义从来不取决于结果。
```

The scene already did the work. The explanation doesn't deepen — it flattens. It tells the reader what to feel after the writing already made them feel it.

Variants:
- 也许这就是… ("perhaps this is…")
- 那一刻我突然明白… ("in that moment I suddenly understood…")
- 这不是X——是Y ("this isn't X — it's Y")
- 我觉得… ("I think…") [when used as a thesis-wrap]

These aren't always wrong. Sometimes the explicit statement is the point. But in this corpus, they're default, not chosen. The writer reaches for them out of habit, not need.

### 2. THE PERFECT PLAYER

In dialogues, the "correct" answer is consistently a mini-essay of poetic insight. No real person responds like this:

> "地铁窗户上的倒影很有意思——你看到的是自己，但背景一直在变。好像在提醒你，人还是那个人，但每一天都不一样。"

This is a late-night subway ride with a stranger. Nobody speaks in polished metaphors to someone they met two minutes ago. The correct answers consistently sound like the best paragraph from a literary essay, not like speech.

This creates two problems:
- It makes every dialogue feel like a philosophy seminar
- It teaches the learner that "good" Chinese = poetic abstraction, when actually good Chinese conversation often means: warmth, curiosity, humor, and social attunement

### 3. THE CARTOONISH WRONG ANSWER

Wrong dialogue options are almost always absurdly off-topic:

- "How much does your recorder cost?" (during a discussion of cultural memory)
- "Can I try playing it?" (when the instrument doesn't exist yet)
- "How much can a guqin sell for?" (during a meditation on time and wood)

Real wrong answers should be *socially plausible but less engaged.* A person who asks about the recorder brand isn't an alien — they're just not on the same wavelength. But as written, wrong answers make the chooser seem incompetent at being human.

Better wrong answers would be:
- A reasonable response that's emotionally shallow
- A topic-adjacent response that shows less attunement
- A good-faith response that misses the specific register

### 4. MONOTONAL ARCHITECTURE

Nearly every passage follows the same shape:

```
observation → sensory detail → sensory detail → reflection → moral/realization
```

The wet market piece ("How a Wet Market Works") breaks this pattern with system-explanation architecture. It's one of the strongest in the corpus partly because of that structural difference.

Missing architectures:
- argument → complication → revised argument
- scene → scene → juxtaposition (no narrator comment)
- character portrait through accumulated habits
- two-voice dialogue essay
- escalation through a small social conflict
- list/catalog that builds pressure through accumulation
- in medias res → context → return to opening with changed meaning
- question → investigation → partial answer

### 5. SINGLE EMOTIONAL REGISTER

The corpus is overwhelmingly wistful/contemplative. This is a real strength in moderation — it gives the app a coherent mood. But at current saturation, it becomes monotonal.

Approximate emotional distribution in what I've read:
- ~60% wistful observation / quiet beauty
- ~15% gentle warmth / tenderness
- ~10% mild wonder
- ~10% nostalgic reflection
- ~5% everything else

What's almost entirely missing:
- social comedy / awkwardness
- friction / disagreement
- institutional competence / system satisfaction
- raw food pleasure (not food-as-metaphor — food as messy, beloved eating)
- defiance / resistance to pomposity
- embarrassment
- small-scale social conflict
- deadpan humor
- people being wrong in human ways

### 6. RETRIEVAL QUESTIONS DISGUISED AS COMPREHENSION

Most questions at all levels ask "what did the text say?" with answer wording lifted directly from the passage. Even at HSK 7-9, inference is rare.

Example (HSK 7, "Woman Who Waters Dead Plants"):
- Q: "Why does she water dead plants?"
- A: "The act keeps her connected to her deceased husband" ← this is basically a direct quote

Better question: "What did the neighbors get wrong about the woman?"
This requires the reader to synthesize the contrast between the neighbors' theories (she's mentally ill / she's lazy) and the actual reason (ritual as connection). Same information, but the reader has to do interpretive work.

### 7. TRANSLATION AS SUPPORT COPY

English translations are generally accurate but often add small explanatory cushions the Chinese didn't need, or flatten rhythm and register.

Example:
- Chinese: 椅子在，人不在。
- Current English: "the chairs are there, but the people aren't."
- The Chinese is six characters. Two parallel clauses. No conjunction. Stark.
- The "but" softens the contrast the Chinese deliberately withheld.

Better: "The chairs are there. The people aren't."

## PART A: FIVE B-LEVEL → A-LEVEL PROSE REVISIONS

### A1. "The Locksmith's Afternoon" (j4_observe_001)

**Current ending (B-level — earned but familiar):**
> 那些安静的下午，他和这个小区一起慢慢变老。

**Problem:** The "growing old together" metaphor is warm but generic. It wraps up too neatly. The piece already earned its feeling through the tools-on-cloth and children-watching-keys details.

**Revised ending:**
> 那些安静的下午，他有时候会把一把旧钥匙拿起来看半天——不是因为有人要配，只是因为它的形状好看。

*("On those quiet afternoons, sometimes he picks up an old key and looks at it for a long time — not because someone needs a copy, but because its shape is beautiful.")*

**Why this is stronger:** It ends on a specific image. It reveals character (he finds beauty in his own trade). It doesn't announce the theme — it *is* the theme. The reader arrives at the feeling of "growing old with quiet purpose" without being told.

---

### A2. "The Street Sweeper's Dawn" (j4_observe_022)

**Current ending (B-level — competent metaphor, over-explained):**
> 她就像一个隐形的人，在别人看不到的时间里，默默地把城市擦亮了。

**Problem:** "Invisible person" and "silently polishing the city" are both metaphors that explain the point the scene already made. Two metaphors for the same idea = redundancy.

**Revised ending:**
> 早上出门的人走在干净的街道上，大部分人不会低头看地面。她也不会抬头看他们。

*("People heading out in the morning walk on clean streets; most don't look down at the ground. She doesn't look up at them either.")*

**Why this is stronger:** The parallel structure (they don't look down / she doesn't look up) creates a social geometry that implies the whole class dynamic without stating it. No metaphor needed. The restraint *is* the meaning.

---

### A3. "The Sound of Falling Leaves" (j9_quiet_101)

**Current ending (B-level — explicit moral):**
> 也许这就是自然教给我们的最安静的一课：放手不是失去，而是为下一次拥有腾出空间。

**Problem:** This is the corpus's most common tic — the 也许这就是 + lesson formula. The ginkgo image (six to eight seconds, golden fan, sway) is strong enough to carry the ending alone.

**Revised ending:**
> 我在银杏树下站了很久。地上已经铺了一层金色。新落下的叶子覆盖在旧叶子上面，像一封一封没有打开过的信。

*("I stood under the ginkgo for a long time. The ground was already covered in gold. New leaves falling over old ones, like letters never opened.")*

**Why this is stronger:** The image (letters never opened) creates resonance without declaring meaning. It has a quiet ache — the sense of accumulation, of things left unread or unreceived. The reader can interpret: is it loss? is it natural abundance? is it acceptance? The ambiguity is more alive than the thesis.

---

### A4. "The Afternoon Light on an Old Wall" (j9_observe_001)

**Current (B-level — strong until the last two sentences):**
> 那一刻我突然明白，所谓现代化，有时候不过是用一层涂料抹去几十年的对话。

**Problem:** 那一刻我突然明白 is the exact realization-crutch the editorial rules flag. And "a coat of paint erasing decades of dialogue" — while a strong idea — is stated as epigram. The scene already made the point: wall painted white = dialogue erased. The narrator doesn't need to editorialize.

**Revised ending:**
> 有一天我路过，发现墙被刷成了白色。施工的人在收拾工具。下午三点的阳光照在白墙上，什么痕迹都没有了——干干净净的。

*("One day I passed by and found the wall had been painted white. The workers were packing up their tools. The three o'clock sunlight fell on the white wall — no traces at all. Spotless.")*

**Why this is stronger:** "干干净净的" (spotless) does devastating double work — it's what the workers would say approvingly, and what the narrator feels as loss. The irony is structural, not declared. And returning to "three o'clock sunlight" — the exact time the light-band appeared — makes the absence concrete. The reader realizes what's gone without being told.

### A5. "The Woman Who Waters Dead Plants" (j7_observe_001)

**Current ending (B-level — the last paragraph over-explains an A-level scene):**
> 我忽然明白，仪式的意义从来不取决于结果。枯萎的花盆里盛放的不是植物，而是一段不肯散场的记忆。水浇下去，蒸发了也好，渗进土里也罢，那个弯腰、倾壶、等待的姿势才是真正在生长的东西。

**Problem:** The woman's own words — "浇水这个动作本身就够了" ("the act of watering itself is enough") — already contain the entire meaning. Every sentence after that repeats it in increasingly literary language. The narrator should trust her, and trust the reader.

**Revised ending (cut after her dialogue, add one line):**
> 她笑了笑说：「这些花是我丈夫在世的时候种的。他走了以后我试过重新种，可怎么也养不活。后来我想，浇水这个动作本身就够了——它让我每天傍晚还能站在这里，做一件跟他有关的事。」
>
> 那天之后，我再也没有觉得那些花是枯死的。

*("After that day, I never again thought of those flowers as dead.")*

**Why this is stronger:** One sentence. It tells you the narrator's perception changed without explaining how or why. The reader has already done the emotional work — they understand. The single-line ending lets the feeling settle instead of elaborating it into abstraction.

## PART B: FIVE FORMULAIC ENDINGS → EARNED ENDINGS

### B1. j7_observe_051 — "The Color of Five O'Clock"

**Current:** 「美」不是稀缺的，美只是需要你在正确的时刻抬一下头。
**Problem:** Thesis statement as ending. Announces what the piece implies.
**Revised:** 今天五点零三分，我抬头看了一眼。颜色已经开始变了。
*("Today at 5:03, I looked up. The color was already changing.")*
**Why better:** Specificity (5:03, not "around five"). The transience — "already changing" — embodies the point about fleeting beauty more than the declaration does. Shows the narrator practicing the habit, not preaching it.

### B2. j4_inst_003 — "The Post Office Counter"

**Current:** 只要还有人寄信，邮局就不会关门。
**Problem:** Tidy moral bow. Also, probably not true — post offices close for economic reasons regardless.
**Revised:** 我走出来的时候，那个老人正在舔信封。他舔得很慢，很认真，好像这个动作本身就是信的一部分。
*("When I walked out, the old man was licking an envelope. Slowly, carefully, as if the act itself were part of the letter.")*
**Why better:** Concrete image. The slowness of licking mirrors the slowness of handwriting, of refusing digital. The body (tongue, paper, glue) replaces the abstraction. And "as if the act itself were part of the letter" echoes the watering-dead-plants theme — ritual as content — without repeating it.

### B4. j7_observe_022 — "Rain on the Tin Roof"

**Current:** 也许有一天这栋老房子会被拆掉，但我会永远记得这种声音。
**Problem:** Anticipatory nostalgia + "I'll always remember" = formula. The sentence does nothing the piece hasn't already done.
**Revised:** 今天早上雨停了以后，屋顶滴了很久的水。每一滴之间的间隔越来越长，直到最后一滴落下来的时候，安静得像是有人轻轻把门关上了。

**Why better:** The rain piece should end with rain, not with a meta-statement about memory. "Someone gently closing a door" — ambiguous: is it an ending? a departure? simple physics? The image does the emotional work the declaration was trying to do.

## PART C: FIVE STRUCTURALLY REPETITIVE HSK 7-9 ESSAYS REWRITTEN WITH DIFFERENT ARCHITECTURES

### C1. "The Weight of Slippers" (j7_observe_010)

**Current architecture:** observation → backstory → mother's explanation → metaphor → tidy resolution
**Problem:** The piece follows the standard discovery arc. Object → mystery → someone explains → narrator understands.

**Rewritten with ACCUMULATION architecture (details pile up until the weight becomes unbearable):**

> 父亲住院以后，我回家整理他的东西。
>
> 鞋架上的棕色拖鞋，鞋底磨出两个凹痕。左脚鞋带断过，用鱼线缝的，针脚粗得像在缝帆布袋。
>
> 衣柜里三件差不多的深蓝色外套。最旧的那件领口起了毛球，但拉链是好的。
>
> 茶杯旁边一个小本子，记着每天的血压。字越来越大——不是因为他想写大字，是因为他看不清了。
>
> 厨房抽屉里一把生锈的剪刀，一卷用了一半的透明胶带，四根橡皮筋，一个空的药盒。
>
> 我坐在客厅里，对面是空的沙发。拖鞋还在门口，凹痕朝上，像两只等着被穿的手。
>
> 我没有收起来。

*("After my father was hospitalized, I went home to sort his things. // Brown slippers on the shoe rack, soles worn into two indentations. Left strap broke once, sewn with fishing line — stitches coarse as if mending a canvas bag. // Three nearly identical dark blue jackets in the closet. The oldest one had pilling at the collar, but the zipper still worked. // A small notebook by the teacup, recording daily blood pressure. The characters got bigger and bigger — not because he wanted to write large, but because he couldn't see clearly anymore. // A rusty pair of scissors in the kitchen drawer, a half-used roll of tape, four rubber bands, an empty pill box. // I sat in the living room. The sofa across from me was empty. The slippers were still by the door, indentations facing up, like two hands waiting to be worn. // I didn't put them away.")*

**What changed:** No mother's explanation. No metaphor about "eight years of gravity." Just objects. The accumulation — the blood-pressure notebook with characters getting bigger — does more emotional damage than any declared insight. The reader assembles the father from his things. The ending ("I didn't put them away") is the same, but now it lands harder because nothing has been explained.

### C2. "The Language of Neon" (j9_city_026)

**Current architecture:** comparison → sensory description → atmospheric peak → rhetorical question
**Problem:** The comparison (LED vs. neon) is strong, but the piece unfolds as a lecture with mood breaks. The ending rhetorical question ("have we cleared away the city's poetry?") is an essay-exam conclusion.

**Rewritten with THEN/NOW JUXTAPOSITION architecture (two scenes, no commentary):**

> 2003年。南京路。晚上十点。我从补习班出来，骑自行车回家。整条路都是霓虹灯——红的、蓝的、绿的，每一个字都在微微颤抖，好像它们不是灯管，是活的东西在呼吸。下过雨的地面把所有的颜色翻了一倍。我骑在两层霓虹灯之间——抬头是一层，低头是一层。鞋底是湿的。空气里有烧烤的烟。
>
> 2024年。南京路。晚上十点。LED屏幕滚动着广告，每一帧都高清、精准、亮得刺眼。地面是干的。我走过去的时候，屏幕换了三遍画面，但我一个都没记住。

*("2003. Nanjing Road. 10 PM. I came out of cram school, biked home. The whole road was neon — red, blue, green, every character trembling slightly, as if they weren't tubes but living things breathing. The rain-washed ground doubled every color. I biked between two layers of neon — one above, one below. My shoes were wet. The air smelled like barbecue smoke. // 2024. Nanjing Road. 10 PM. LED screens scrolled advertisements, every frame high-definition, precise, blindingly bright. The ground was dry. When I walked past, the screen changed images three times, but I didn't remember a single one.")*

**What changed:** No argument about "the city's poetry." No comparison essay. Just two scenes, twenty years apart. The reader feels the loss through contrast: wet shoes vs. dry ground, trembling vs. precise, two layers of neon vs. forgettable screens. The barbecue smoke — a detail that has nothing to do with light — anchors the first scene in sensory reality. The second scene is deliberately flat. The flatness is the point.

### C3. "The Archaeology of a Name" (j9_identity_051)

**Current architecture:** thesis → chronological survey (1950s→2000s) → personal anecdote → restatement
**Problem:** The chronological name-survey is interesting content but textbook architecture. It reads like an essay rather than something discovered through living.

**Rewritten with PERSONAL MYSTERY architecture (start with a specific problem, widen):**

> 我一直不喜欢自己的名字。「建明」——太普通了。我们年级有三个建明。小学的时候老师叫「建明」，三个人同时抬头。
>
> 我问过父亲为什么给我起这个名字。他说：「你出生的时候是早上五点，天刚亮。建明——建设光明。」我觉得这个解释太大了。一个刚出生的婴儿，身上就背着「建设光明」四个字，像一份没有签过的合同。
>
> 后来我查了一下我们班的花名册。1983年出生的男生里，带「建」字的有七个，带「明」字的有五个，带「伟」字的有四个。我们不是被命名——我们是被一个时代统一编号了。
>
> 但我女儿叫「雨桐」。我给她起名字的时候，在笔记本上写了四十多个备选。我想了整整一个月。到最后我选了「雨桐」，不是因为它最好听，而是因为我想到了一棵梧桐树在雨里的样子——安静、不着急、该长多高就长多高。
>
> 一个月以后我发现，她幼儿园的班上还有两个「雨桐」。

*("I never liked my own name. 'Jianming' — too ordinary. Three Jianmings in our grade. When the teacher called 'Jianming' in elementary school, three people looked up at the same time. // I asked my father why he chose it. He said: 'You were born at five in the morning, just as the sky was getting light. Jianming — building brightness.' I thought the explanation was too grand. A newborn baby, already carrying 'building brightness' on his back, like a contract he never signed. // Later I checked our class roster. Among boys born in 1983, seven had 建 in their names, five had 明, four had 伟. We weren't named — we were serial-numbered by an era. // But my daughter is called 'Yutong.' When I named her, I wrote over forty candidates in a notebook. I thought about it for a whole month. In the end I chose 'Yutong,' not because it sounded the best, but because I thought of a wutong tree in the rain — quiet, unhurried, growing as tall as it's meant to grow. // A month later, I discovered there were two other 'Yutongs' in her kindergarten class.")*

**What changed:** The chronological survey becomes a personal story. Same information (era-specific naming patterns, 1950s→2000s shift, the irony of individuality producing homogeneity) but delivered through one person's experience. The ending — his daughter shares her "unique" name with two classmates — is a punchline that replaces the thesis. The humor is real, warm, and self-deprecating. The reader gets the cultural analysis without being lectured.

### C4. "The Fermentation of Time" (j9_food_076)

**Current architecture:** thesis → examples → grandmother anecdote → explicit contrast (time vs. industry) → declared meaning
This piece is already B+/A- — the grandmother's soy sauce is strong. But the framing is essay-mode.

**Rewritten with RECIPE ARCHITECTURE (structured as instructions, subverts format):**

> 外婆的酱油。做法如下。
>
> 春天。煮大豆。摊在竹匾上。搬到阳台，晒。等豆子表面长出一层白色的霉——不要害怕，这是对的。
>
> 把豆子封进陶缸。加盐。加水。盖上盖子。
>
> 每天早上打开缸盖，搅一次。让阳光照进去。然后盖上。第二天再打开，再搅。再盖上。
>
> 重复。一整个夏天。
>
> 九月的时候打开缸盖。里面的液体已经变成深褐色，稠的，有一种你在超市里闻不到的香味——因为这种香味需要三个月的阳光才能长出来。
>
> 舀进玻璃瓶里。贴上日期。放进柜子。这就是酱油。
>
> 整个过程没有任何添加剂。唯一的添加剂是时间。你不能加快它。你试过加快它。你用的是超市酱油。味道是对的——化学上是对的。但是少了什么。不是少了味道。是少了那个每天早上打开缸盖搅一下再盖上的人。

*("Grandmother's soy sauce. Instructions as follows. // Spring. Boil soybeans. Spread on bamboo trays. Carry to the balcony. Sun-dry. Wait for a white mold to grow on the surface — don't be afraid, this is correct. // Seal the beans in a clay urn. Add salt. Add water. Cover. // Every morning, open the lid. Stir once. Let sunlight in. Cover again. Next day, open again. Stir again. Cover again. // Repeat. All summer. // In September, open the lid. The liquid inside has turned deep brown, thick, with a fragrance you can't smell in a supermarket — because this fragrance needs three months of sunlight to grow. // Ladle into glass bottles. Label with the date. Put in the cupboard. This is soy sauce. // The entire process has no additives. The only additive is time. You can't speed it up. You've tried speeding it up. You use supermarket soy sauce. The taste is right — chemically right. But something is missing. It's not the flavor that's missing. It's the person who opens the lid every morning, stirs once, and closes it again.")*

**What changed:** The recipe format creates rhythm through repetition. Short sentences. Imperative voice. The shift to second person ("You've tried speeding it up") is sudden and intimate — it implicates the reader. The ending ("It's the person") is the same insight but delivered through accusation, not meditation. It lands harder because the format earned it.

### C5. Entirely new architecture: SYSTEM COMEDY (missing from HSK 7-9 entirely)

There is no piece in the corpus that works as institutional/system comedy. Here's what one could look like:

**"The Queue Theory of Auntie Wang" / 王阿姨的排队理论 (HSK 7)**

> 王阿姨有一套完整的排队理论。她把它叫做「位置学」，虽然没有人叫她这么做。
>
> 第一条：超市最快的收银台不是人最少的那一条——是人最多但每个人的篮子最小的那一条。
>
> 第二条：菜市场最好的摊位不在入口，在从入口数第三个。入口的摊位客人多但蔬菜不是最新鲜的，因为老板知道你会因为方便而妥协。第三个摊位需要靠质量把你拉进去。
>
> 第三条：银行叫号机的秘密是：星期二上午人最少。不是星期一——星期一大家把周末攒的事情都拿来办。也不是星期五——星期五大家急着在周末之前处理完。星期二上午，世界暂时忘记了银行的存在。
>
> 我问她这些理论是怎么总结出来的。她说：「退休以后每天早上没事干，我就在各种队伍里排队观察。排了三年。」
>
> 我又问她：「你为什么不把时间花在别的事情上？」
>
> 她很认真地看着我说：「你觉得这不是正经事？我在研究这个城市的运行规律。这跟科学家没有区别。只是我的实验室是菜市场。」
>
> 我没有反驳。因为她的超市收银台理论，我亲自验证过。是对的。

*("Auntie Wang has a complete theory of queues. She calls it 'positional science,' though nobody asked her to. // Rule one: the fastest supermarket checkout isn't the one with the fewest people — it's the one with the most people but the smallest baskets. // Rule two: the best stall at the wet market isn't at the entrance — it's the third one from the entrance. The entrance stall gets customers but its vegetables aren't the freshest, because the owner knows you'll compromise for convenience. The third stall has to pull you in with quality. // Rule three: the secret of the bank's ticket machine — Tuesday mornings have the fewest people. Not Monday: everyone brings their weekend backlog. ## PART E: FIVE CHINESE → ENGLISH TRANSLATION IMPROVEMENTS

### E1. j1_urban_012 — "Two Chairs on the Sidewalk"

**Current:** "Today it rained — the chairs are there, but the people aren't."
**Original Chinese:** 今天下雨了，椅子在，人不在。
**Problem:** The "but" adds a conjunction the Chinese deliberately omitted. The dash before "the chairs" also softens the transition.
**Revised:** "Today it rained. The chairs are there. The people aren't."
**Why better:** Three sentences. The terseness matches the Chinese. The period after "today it rained" lets the reader feel the change before learning what changed.

### E2. j7_observe_001 — "The Woman Who Waters Dead Plants"

**Current:** "I suddenly understood that the meaning of ritual never depends on outcome."
**Original Chinese:** 我忽然明白，仪式的意义从来不取决于结果。
**Problem:** The Chinese line is already B-level (over-explains). But the English makes it worse by being grammatically heavier than the Chinese. If we're keeping the line, at least make the English lighter.
**Revised (if keeping the line):** "I understood then: ritual doesn't need to work."
**Why better:** Shorter, plainer, more confident. "Doesn't need to work" is more colloquial and more devastating than "the meaning of ritual never depends on outcome."

### E3. j4_observe_001 — "The Locksmith's Afternoon"

**Current:** "as if preparing a small exhibition"
**Original Chinese:** 像在准备一场小小的展览
**Problem:** Not wrong, but "exhibition" is slightly formal in English. The Chinese 小小的展览 is deliberately humble — a *little* exhibition.
**Revised:** "like he's setting up a little show"
**Why better:** "Setting up" is more physical than "preparing." "A little show" matches the tenderness of 小小的展览 — humble, slightly proud, not taking itself too seriously.

### E4. j4_urban_091 — "Convenience Store at 3 AM"

**Current:** "I think the city at 3 AM is especially honest — no decoration, no performance, just the most authentic quiet."
**Original Chinese:** 我觉得凌晨三点的城市特别诚实，没有装饰，没有表演，只有最真实的安静。
**Problem:** "The most authentic quiet" is clunky in English. 最真实的安静 is simpler than it sounds — it just means: what's left when everyone stops pretending.
**Revised:** "I think the city at three in the morning is honest. No decoration. No performance. Just quiet that has nothing to prove."
**Why better:** "Quiet that has nothing to prove" is an English sentence. "The most authentic quiet" is a translated Chinese sentence. The former creates an image; the latter describes a concept.

### E5. j9_food_076 — "The Fermentation of Time"

**Current:** "In an era obsessed with speed, the very existence of these foods is a provocation — they say: 'You can accelerate everything, but you cannot accelerate me.'"
**Original Chinese:** 在一个追求速度的时代，这些食物的存在本身就是一种挑衅——它们在说：「你可以加速一切，但你加速不了我。」
**Problem:** "The very existence" and "is a provocation" are accurate but heavy. The Chinese reads more naturally — the personification (foods "talking") is playful.
**Revised:** "In an age that worships speed, these foods are an affront. They're saying: 'Speed up whatever you want. You can't speed me up.'"
**Why better:** Two sentences instead of one. "An affront" is punchier than "a provocation." The direct speech is colloquial ("speed up whatever you want") rather than formal ("you can accelerate everything"). It sounds like someone actually talking back.

## PART G: SUMMARY PRIORITIES

### What to do first:
1. **Cut explained morals** from HSK 7-9 passages. This is the single highest-impact change. ~40% of pieces end this way. Most can end 1-3 sentences earlier, or replace the explanation with a returning image.

2. **Rewrite dialogue feedback lines.** Every feedback line should be the NPC's reaction in scene, not editorial commentary on the player's answer. This is a mechanical pass — time-consuming but not creatively difficult.

3. **Diversify wrong answers.** Replace cartoonishly off-topic wrong answers with socially plausible but less attuned responses. The wrong answers should make the learner think, not laugh at a straw man.

4. **Add missing tonal registers.** The corpus needs at least 15-20% more social comedy, institutional texture, and friction. These can be new pieces or rewrites of weaker existing ones.

5. **Upgrade questions.** Especially at HSK 1-3, where almost all questions are pure retrieval. Even simple questions can require small inferences.

6. **Vary architecture at HSK 7-9.** The observation → detail → reflection → moral shape needs to be broken. System pieces, character portraits, juxtaposition, recipe formats, dialogue essays, comedy — all viable alternatives.

7. **Tighten translations.** A targeted pass on all English to remove added conjunctions, flatten over-formal constructions, and match the Chinese register.

### What to protect:
- The HSK 1 vignettes are often genuinely good. "The Dog That Sits," "Two Chairs on the Sidewalk," "Who Opened the Window?" — clean, simple, emotionally clear. Don't over-revise.
- "How a Wet Market Works" — one of the strongest pieces. Protect its architecture.
- "The Extra Coffee" — good narrative tension, good reveal.
- The grandmother's soy sauce in "Fermentation of Time" — strong specificity. The detail should survive even if the framing changes.
- The j9 guqin dialogue has genuinely strong ideas. The problem is in the delivery format (perfect-player, cartoonish-wrong), not the intellectual content.
